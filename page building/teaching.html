Medical Sociology
Spring 2025 | University at Albany, SUNY | Instructor

This course introduces students to sociological perspectives on health, illness, and healthcare systems. We examine how social factors such as race, class, gender, and age influence patterns of health and illness, as well as access to healthcare. The course covers topics including social determinants of health, healthcare inequalities, medical institutions, and the social construction of illness.

Throughout the semester, students engage with theoretical frameworks and empirical research to understand how social structures shape individual and population health outcomes. We also discuss contemporary debates in healthcare policy and practice from a sociological perspective.

Learning Outcomes:

  • Analyze how social factors and structural inequalities shape health outcomes
  • Apply sociological theories to understand healthcare systems and practices
  • Critically evaluate research on social determinants of health
  • Develop skills in analyzing health data and policies from a sociological perspective

Statistics for Sociologists
Spring 2023 | University at Albany, SUNY | Co-Teacher

This course introduces students to statistical methods and data analysis techniques commonly used in sociological research. Students learn how to analyze social data using statistical software, interpret results, and present findings effectively. The course covers descriptive statistics, probability, hypothesis testing, correlation, regression analysis, and an introduction to advanced techniques.

Through hands-on exercises and research projects, students develop practical skills in quantitative data analysis and learn to apply statistical concepts to real-world sociological questions. Special emphasis is placed on critical interpretation of statistical findings and understanding the limitations of quantitative methods.

Learning Outcomes:

  • Apply appropriate statistical techniques to analyze sociological data
  • Interpret statistical results in the context of sociological theories
  • Develop proficiency in statistical software commonly used in sociology
  • Design and execute basic quantitative research projects
  • Critically evaluate quantitative research in sociological literature

Teaching Philosophy

My teaching philosophy is grounded in creating an inclusive, engaging, and intellectually rigorous learning environment. I believe that effective teaching in sociology requires not only conveying theoretical knowledge and methodological skills but also fostering critical thinking about social structures and inequalities. My approach emphasizes three core principles:

1. Connecting Theory to Lived Experiences
I strive to make sociological concepts accessible by connecting abstract theories to students' lived experiences and contemporary social issues. By demonstrating the relevance of sociological perspectives to everyday life, I help students develop a deeper understanding of how social structures shape individual experiences and how individuals can influence social change.

2. Fostering Critical Inquiry
My courses are designed to help students develop skills in critical inquiry and analysis. Through diverse readings, discussion-based activities, and research projects, I encourage students to question assumptions, evaluate evidence, and develop well-reasoned arguments. I believe that these skills are essential not only for academic success but also for engaged citizenship.

3. Creating Inclusive Learning Communities
I am committed to creating inclusive learning environments that respect diverse perspectives and experiences. I incorporate diverse voices and viewpoints in course materials, implement teaching strategies that accommodate different learning styles, and establish classroom norms that promote respectful dialogue. By cultivating a sense of belonging and intellectual community, I aim to empower all students to participate fully in the learning process.

In my role as an educator, I see myself as both a guide and a co-learner. I provide structure, feedback, and expertise while remaining open to new perspectives and approaches. My ultimate goal is to inspire students to become lifelong learners who apply sociological insights to understand and address social problems.
